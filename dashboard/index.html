Why this matters & what we're asking start here if you're new to the project

Motivation

Parkinson's disease (PD) is the 2nd most common neurodegenerative disorder (~10M people). It's usually diagnosed late, from motor symptoms that appear only after large neuron loss. EEG — recording the brain's electrical activity with scalp electrodes — is cheap, non-invasive, and widely available. If a model could flag PD from EEG, it could enable earlier, cheaper screening. But to be clinically useful, it must work on patients from a different hospital than it was trained on — different machines, different patients. That "works across sites" property is the real bar, and most prior work doesn't test it.

Research questions

  • RQ1. If we train on EEG from some hospitals and test on a held-out hospital, can the model still detect PD? (real-world generalization)
  • RQ2. Does self-supervised pretraining on a large pile of unlabeled EEG make that cross-hospital transfer better?
  • RQ3. Is the high accuracy reported in this field actually measuring Parkinson's — or something else?
Our answers, in one line: RQ1 — yes, but modestly. RQ2 — no, not at our scale. RQ3 — something else (the hospital).

Key concepts plain-language glossary — Saanvi & Alex, this is your cheat sheet

EEG Electrodes on the scalp record the brain's electrical signals over time. Cheap, non-invasive.
Dataset / "site" Each public dataset is from a different hospital with different equipment. We use 4 labeled PD sets + a big unlabeled clinical corpus (TUH).
Balanced accuracy Accuracy that's fair when classes are imbalanced — the average of "how well it finds PD" and "how well it finds healthy." 0.5 = random guessing.
ROC-AUC Does the model rank PD above healthy, regardless of where you set the cutoff? 0.5 = no signal, 1.0 = perfect. Threshold-independent.
Confound A shortcut that isn't the real thing. Here: "which hospital" almost tells you the label, so a model can "cheat" without learning Parkinson's.
Cross-site / LODO Leave-One-Dataset-Out: train on 3 hospitals, test on the 4th unseen one. The honest test of generalization.
Calibration Choosing the right decision cutoff. A model can rank cases well yet still mislabel everyone if the cutoff is wrong for a new site.
Self-supervised (SSL) Pretrain on lots of unlabeled EEG to learn general features, then fine-tune on the small labeled PD data. The "foundation model" recipe.

The bottom line — three findings

1 · The headline is confounded

A model using zero EEG — just "which dataset is this?" — scores 0.927 on the same pooled protocol, ≥ published models. So the famous high accuracy isn't measuring Parkinson's.

2 · The "failure" is calibration

On an unseen hospital, the model still ranks PD vs healthy (AUC 0.76). It only looked like chance because the cutoff was wrong; a deployable cutoff recovers 0.64 balanced accuracy.

3 · SSL doesn't help (yet)

Self-supervised pretraining (even on huge disjoint TUH) and data augmentation give no cross-site lift — confirmed across 3 evaluation modes. A clean, honest negative.

Finding 1 — A no-EEG model beats the published numbers

Pooled-protocol balanced accuracy site-confounded

How to read it: the red bar is a "dumb" baseline that never looks at the brain signal — it only knows which dataset a recording is from and guesses that dataset's most common label. It ties the best models. That's only possible because one dataset (ds004148) is 100% healthy and makes up 66% of the data, so "which dataset" ≈ "the answer."

Why it happens

  • 4 datasets are pooled; we hold out subjects but never whole hospitals — so the "which hospital" shortcut is always available.
  • EEG hospitals are easy to tell apart (amplifier, montage, filtering).
  • The model can ace the test by recognizing the hospital, not the disease.
  • The fix: always report this null, and test by holding out a whole hospital (LODO).

The lesson: with the default cutoff (0.5) the model predicts "PD" for almost everyone on a new hospital → looks like chance. But its ranking is good (AUC 0.76). Just choosing the cutoff on the training hospitals ("train-transferred", fully deployable) recovers real accuracy. Cross-site failure here is a calibration problem, not a "no signal" problem.

SSL is supposed to shine when labels are scarce. It doesn't here: TUH-init ≈ from-scratch at every budget (bands overlap, scratch nominally ahead) — robust across 3 seeds. Supervised reaches ~0.70 AUC by 25% of labels; SSL doesn't lower that. (Cross-site AUC; higher = better.)

How our understanding evolved this is what real research looks like

Where we started
Hypothesis: self-supervised pretraining on cross-hospital EEG would beat the state of the art and generalize. Early pooled numbers looked great (~84–92%). The plan was "improve the number."
What we found by digging
That number is inflated by a hidden site confound (a no-EEG model scores 0.93). On the honest cross-hospital test it collapsed — and the collapse turned out to be a fixable calibration issue. SSL didn't help.
Why this is a win
Exposing a field-wide measurement flaw, giving an honest cross-site baseline, and reporting a careful negative is a stronger, more publishable contribution than another inflated score. Good science follows the evidence — even when it overturns the hypothesis.
